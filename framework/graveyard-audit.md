# Framework Validation — Graveyard Audit

## Purpose

Eliminate survivorship bias from the 40-year stress test. The framework was validated against companies that succeeded. This audit stress-tests it against companies that *looked like quality* at peak but subsequently destroyed capital.

> **Core question for each case:** At what point would the 6-Phase Framework have triggered an exit, and which phase catches it?

**Rule:** If a case below would NOT have been caught by the framework as written, a gap exists that must be patched before it recurs in a live holding.

**Cadence:** Review annually in Q1. Add 3 new cases per year.

## What This Protects Against

The 40-year stress test was built using companies visible and successful today. This creates a structural blind spot: it only confirms the framework catches winners — it does not prove the framework avoids losers. A framework that scores MSFT and META correctly but misses Valeant and Wirecard provides false confidence.

## Case Library

### Case 1 — General Electric (GE)

**Peak quality appearance:** ROIC 15%+, AAA-rated, decades of earnings growth, "most admired company" for years running.

**What broke:** GE Capital — a financial subsidiary comprising ~50% of earnings — was carrying enormous hidden leverage invisible in reported industrial metrics. When 2008 hit, it required a near-bailout.

**Destruction period:** 2008–2018 · Peak to trough: −85%

**Which phase catches it:**
- Phase 01 (Balance Sheet): Consolidating GE Capital's debt pushed Net Debt/EBITDA to 6–8×, well above the 2× threshold — catchable as early as 2003.
- Phase 04 (Monitoring): The "debt rises" quality flag should have triggered as GE Capital grew relative to industrial earnings.

**Gap identified → fixed:** Conglomerate structures with captive financial subsidiaries require consolidated debt analysis (now the "Conglomerate rule" in Phase 01).

### Case 2 — Nokia

**Peak quality appearance:** 40%+ gross margins, dominant global brand, genuine network effects, 40%+ global mobile market share (2007).

**What broke:** TAM disruption. iPhone (2007) and Android (2008) rendered Nokia's software stack irrelevant. Revenue CAGR went negative by 2009.

**Destruction period:** 2007–2012 · Peak to trough: −95%

**Which phase catches it:**
- Phase 06 (Exit): "TAM shrinking, disruption visible, pricing power lost" — all three visible by 2009.
- Phase 04 (Monitoring): Revenue CAGR broke below 10% by Q1 2009.

**Gap identified → fixed:** Moat analysis must include a disruption vector check. Strong current moats do not prevent new-platform disruption — now Qualitative Question 6: "Could a new delivery mechanism, platform, or technology make this moat irrelevant within 5 years?"

### Case 3 — Cisco Systems (Bubble Entry, 2000)

**Peak quality appearance:** Revenue CAGR 40%+, gross margins 65%+, genuine enterprise infrastructure moat. Fundamentals were real.

**What broke:** Not the business — the valuation. At peak (March 2000), Cisco traded at 130× forward earnings. The stock fell 85% despite the business remaining intact.

**Destruction period:** 2000–2002 · Peak to trough: −85%

**Which phase catches it:**
- Phase 02 (Valuation Scoring): FCF yield ~0.5%, EV/EBIT ~80×, PEG >5 → every metric scores 9–10 → Phase 03 blocks entry.
- Phase 05 (Trimming): Any position built at rational 1997–1998 valuations should have been trimmed to tracking size by 1999.

**Gap identified:** None. Full validation — the framework as written would have excluded or trimmed Cisco before the crash. Confirms trim-on-score-expansion logic is structurally correct.

### Case 4 — Valeant Pharmaceuticals

**Peak quality appearance:** Gross margins ~70%, strong reported revenue growth, ROIC appeared high. Favoured by respected value managers including Ackman and the Sequoia Fund.

**What broke:** Accounting manipulation — revenue inflated through a related-party pharmacy network (Philidor). Organic growth near zero. Actual leverage ~8× EBITDA.

**Destruction period:** 2015–2017 · Peak to trough: −95%

**Which phase catches it:**
- Phase 01 (Balance Sheet): Acquisition-driven growth with rising goodwill is a red flag.
- Phase 02 (Qualitative Q1): "Why are margins high?" — Valeant's answer was drug repricing, not pricing power. Deeply unsatisfying.
- Phase 04 (Monitoring): FCF/Net Income conversion was deteriorating — a classic early fraud signal.

**Gap identified → fixed:** Now codified as the FCF Quality Check: if FCF < 70% of reported Net Income for 2+ consecutive years without capex explanation, flag for deep review (Phase 01 + Phase 04), and recalculate organic revenue CAGR separately from acquisition-driven growth (Phase 04 — Organic revenue check).

### Case 5 — Peloton

**Peak quality appearance:** Revenue CAGR >100% (2020–2021), subscription gross margins ~43%, exceptional NPS, strong brand, community network effects.

**What broke:** TAM was COVID-inflated. As gyms reopened, demand collapsed. Fixed manufacturing costs created operating leverage in reverse. FCF was negative throughout the entire growth phase.

**Destruction period:** 2021–2022 · Peak to trough: −97%

**Which phase catches it:**
- Phase 01 (Quality Gate): FCF negative throughout growth — fails "FCF positive for 3 consecutive years." Never enters the Qualified Quality List.
- Phase 02 (P/S vs. Margin): P/S ~20× vs. threshold of ~21.5% (gross margin × 0.5). Score would be 10 immediately.

**Gap identified:** None. Full validation — the FCF-positive screen excludes Peloton entirely. Confirms this gate must not be relaxed under any circumstances.

### Case 6 — Wirecard

**Peak quality appearance:** Revenue growth 30%+/yr, reported net margins ~20%, payments network moat, DAX 30 constituent, EY-audited.

**What broke:** Fraud. €1.9B in "cash" did not exist. Revenue was fabricated. Auditors accepted management representations without independent verification.

**Destruction period:** June 2020 · −99% in days

**Which phase catches it:**
- Phase 01 / Phase 04 (FCF conversion): FCF consistently lagged reported net income — the "cash" never converted because it was fictitious. Same signal as Valeant.
- Phase 04 (Monitoring): Credible FT short-seller reports flagged discrepancies years before collapse.

**Gap identified → fixed:**
1. FCF/Net Income conversion ratio < 70% flag (same as Valeant, now standard).
2. Now codified as Phase 04's "Short thesis engagement": a credible short thesis is not a reason to automatically hold — it is a reason to independently re-verify the specific claim before maintaining or adding to a position.

### Case 7 — IBM

**Peak quality appearance:** Legendary enterprise moat, switching costs, consulting + hardware + software integration, consistent dividends and buybacks for decades.

**What broke:** Growth thesis eroded slowly as cloud computing displaced IBM's on-premise infrastructure. Revenue declined every quarter 2012–2018. Management masked deterioration through aggressive buybacks that inflated EPS while revenue fell.

**Destruction period:** 2012–2022 · Flat to down while S&P 500 tripled

**Which phase catches it:**
- Phase 01 (Growth): Revenue CAGR turned negative by 2013–2014, breaking the >10% threshold.
- Phase 04 (Narrative Check): "Where is growth coming from next 3–5 years?" No satisfying answer by 2014.
- Phase 04 (Earnings quality): EPS growing while revenue falls = buyback-manufactured earnings, not operational growth.

**Gap identified → fixed:** Now codified as Phase 04's "Earnings quality check": if EPS growth exceeds revenue growth by >10pp for 2+ consecutive years, verify operational performance is not masked by buybacks.

## Annual Review Process

Each Q1, add 3 new cases to this library.

**Selection criteria for new cases:**
1. Company that passed (or would have passed) the quality screen at its peak
2. Subsequently lost >50% of value on a permanent basis — not a temporary drawdown
3. Failure mode is different from existing cases in this library

For each case, explicitly answer: **which phase catches it, and how fast?**

If the answer is "no phase catches it" — that is a live framework gap. Patch the relevant phase (and record the change in `decisions/`) before making any new positions.
